Why this is a buying signal for Accenture

Mexican food processors face acute distress with tomato inflation at 107.8% (Jan-Apr 2026), the highest since 1992, creating immediate executive pressure to stabilize supply chains. The combination of 17% US tariffs driving 23.5% export decline, 9% production reduction, climate shocks, and 20% margin compression creates a perfect storm requiring urgent planning transformation. Government coordination efforts and processor pivots to alternative products signal industry-wide recognition that traditional approaches have failed.

Signals to Lead With

→ Extreme price volatility creating executive crisis — Tomato prices nearly doubled year-over-year (99.2%) and reached 107.8% cumulative inflation Jan-Apr 2026, accounting for 90% of Mexico's headline inflation variation and forcing ministerial intervention.
→ Production collapse driving supply chain breakdown — Mexican tomato production forecast down 9% to 2.6 million metric tons with planted area declining 11% (42,112 ha to 38,000 ha) as growers exit amid margin pressure and tariff uncertainty.
→ Trade policy shock accelerating strategic reassessment — 17% US tariff imposed July 2025 drove Q1 2026 exports down 23.5% ($772M to $590M) with Mexico losing 10-12% US market share to Canada, forcing diversification planning and precision agriculture investment.
→ Climate and phytosanitary risks compounding operational fragility — Supply constraints from adverse weather, water shortages, plant disease outbreaks, and combined 20% supply decline in last season highlight vulnerability requiring climate-aware planning capabilities.
